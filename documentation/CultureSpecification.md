# Setting Specification

Version 2.0 | Status: Authoritative

## The Player: An Ancient God

The player is not a character in the world. They are an ancient, unnamed god — a mystical force that existed before humanity and will exist long after. They observe their herd through a **crystal ball**: a precious glass sphere that shows the world below in perfect, luminous detail. All UI is framed as the god's interface with the crystal ball. The grazing screen is a diorama seen through curved glass. The god does not speak to the horses directly. They shape destiny through breeding choices, role assignments, and the slow accumulation of cultural wisdom.

The horses do not know they are watched. They live, grieve, and build culture organically. The god's intervention is invisible — a nudge toward a better pairing, a role assignment that puts the right horse in the right place at the right moment.

This framing is the foundation for all UI, camera, and visual decisions. See RenderingPipelineSpecification.md for the crystal ball camera system and visual implementation.

## The World

**Timeline**: 400+ years after humanity's extinction. The planet has healed completely and then some. Cities have become vertical solarpunk jungles — skyscrapers colonised by vines, solar panels reclaimed by moss, rivers running clear through reclaimed plazas. Nature has not merely returned; it has flourished into something richer than before. Bioluminescent flowers light moonlit trails. Crystal springs bubble up through cracked asphalt. Fruit trees heavy with glowing produce grow from the rooftops of ancient shopping centres.

Ruins are not ruins in the bleak sense. They are **archaeological wonders**, whispered through by old human magic that has degraded into whimsy. A sun-charged amulet grants stamina bursts. A rusted circuit-ring hums ominous chords and curses its wearer with fatigue. These **relic artifacts** are scattered throughout the world, semi-understood, partly dangerous, deeply fascinating.

**Core aesthetic**: Lush, hopeful, health and beauty as virtue. This is not a post-apocalypse. It is a post-humanity renaissance. Tragedy exists — sky burials, Esroh attacks, malnutrition deaths — but even these feel natural and cyclical rather than grim. The world is fundamentally beautiful, and beauty is what the Necrocratic Dragon threatens.

## Sentient Species

Humanity is gone. Several other species have developed varying degrees of sentience and culture in the intervening centuries.

**Horses** form the player's herd. Nomadic, communal, deeply cultural. They are the primary species of the game — not the only intelligent ones, but the ones the god watches.

**Cats** range from housecat philosophers to tiger shamans. They tend rooftop gardens in former cities and guard knowledge accumulated over generations. Cat traders are often the first source of information about the wider world, including early whispers of Kravathax.

**Canine packs** roam urban canyons and reclaimed highways. They trade stories at crossroads and share intelligence freely. In the mid-game they become important allies and sources of information about Necrocratic Dragon activity.

**Whales** are rarely encountered directly but are felt across vast distances. Their telepathic song inspires rituals in herds hundreds of kilometres away. Whale-song events appear as morning events near coastlines. In the late game, whale guidance is instrumental in planning the assault on Kravathax's coastal factories.

Interspecies alliances form naturally around shared threats. No species is inherently hostile to the player's herd. Even the canine packs, who might seem dangerous, operate on their own codes of honour that horses can learn to navigate.

## The Necrocratic Dragon: Kravathax the World-Shaper

**Common name among horse herds**: The Necrocratic Dragon.
**True name**: Kravathax the World-Shaper.

### Appearance

One hundred metres of wingspan. Scales blackened by decades of forge-smoke. Eyes burning industrial orange. He is enormous, deliberate, and ancient — but his most visible presence in the early game is not Kravathax himself but his creations: the **Esrohs**.

### Philosophy

Most dragons in this world are content. They shun sunlight, hoard modest treasures, sleep for decades at a time. Kravathax is the exception. He craves **world domination and permanent fame** with an intensity that other dragons find baffling and horses find terrifying. His philosophy in his own words: *"All shall know my name, or perish unremembered."*

He has rejected the natural beauty of the post-human world entirely. Where others see a solarpunk paradise, he sees wasted potential for industrial output. He is not evil in a simple sense — he is a tyrant with genuine conviction that his way is superior.

### The Esrohs

Kravathax's factories combine salvaged human manufacturing with forbidden dragon-magic to produce **Esroh Golems**: malformed parodies of horses. Jerky metal legs. Exhaust-spewing nostrils. Blades where manes should be. Eyes glowing red. Their movement is uncanny — almost right, then jarringly wrong. Horses experience them as a deep violation: a mockery of life built from the aesthetics of living things.

Esrohs patrol ruined highways, spread industrial smog, and conscript other creatures as factory labour. They are the primary early-game threat. Sentinels detect them. Gladiators fight them. Rebel Culture horses develop specific tactics against them.

### Industrial Spread

Kravathax's mountain factories pump black smoke across solarpunk paradise. Rivers run oily downstream. Solar trees wilt near factory runoff. His ambition is the reconstruction of human-scale industry under dragon rule — all wealth, light, and beauty hoarded for himself. The pollution is not incidental to his plan; it is the plan. A dying world is a controllable world.

## The Three Acts

The game has no mandatory story progression. The three acts describe the **organic arc** that emerges from the mechanics. Players who focus on survival will drift into stabilisation naturally. Culture emerges from repetition, not cutscenes.

### Act 1: Survival (Prey Animals)

The herd flees Esroh smoke through regrown cities. Every morning event is a threat. Malnutrition deaths generate the first journal entries, the first cultural memories. Cat traders in ruins mention "the Black Dragon" in passing. The herd is small, frightened, and beautiful against a hostile but gorgeous world.

Culture is locked to the Survival phase: basic rites only, no diplomacy, no foal XP bonuses. The world feels fragile.

### Act 2: Stabilisation (Culture Emerges)

The herd finds its first stable grazing ground. Storytellers and Priest/ess roles become viable. Solarpunk events begin appearing — solar blooms, crystal springs, fruit windfalls — signalling that the world is on the herd's side if they learn to read it. Canine allies share Dragon intel. Coastal morning events begin including whale-song warnings of new factory construction.

Culture unlocks River and Solar rites. The diplomacy screen opens. Foals are born into herds with accumulated knowledge and arrive with meaningful starting XP. Hope becomes a mechanical resource.

### Act 3: Culture to Legendary (The Great Alliance)

Coalition forms across species. Horse herds, cat shamans, canine packs, and whale-guided assault routes converge on Kravathax's peak. The player's herd leads the final charge — their genetics, their culture, their accumulated journal memories against industrial tyranny.

Culture unlocks multi-trait legendary rites. World map nodes show coalition gathering points. Morning events reference Kravathax by name and describe him actively seeking the player's herd.

## Victory Paths

The endgame approach is determined by the herd's dominant culture. All paths lead to Kravathax's defeat; the method reflects who the herd has become.

| Culture | Victory Path |
|---------|-------------|
| **River** | Sabotage pollution sources → weaken factories → starve Esrohs of materials |
| **Solar** | Solar-charged weapons overload Esroh circuits → burn the factories |
| **Mountain** | Defensive rituals outlast the siege → Esroh desertion without resupply |
| **Plains** | Hit-and-run supply line raids → economic collapse of the factory network |
| **Rebel** | Direct assault on Kravathax → cultural unity breaks his will to fight |

## Thematic Pillars

### Communal Primitivism vs. Industrial Hierarchy

The horse herd embodies communal primitivism: shared labour, collective health, cultural memory over material wealth. No individual horse owns anything. Herd morale is literally the average of every individual. Culture traits emerge from group behaviours, not from any leader's decisions. Foal XP inheritance is the transfer of collective wisdom to the next generation.

Kravathax represents the opposite: hoarding, hierarchy, the suppression of beauty in favour of output. The conflict is not good versus evil — it is a genuinely held philosophical opposition between two ways of organising the world.

### Health and Beauty as Virtue

This is not a world that aestheticises suffering. Coat colours shimmer like oil on water. Communal grooming is a mechanical benefit and a visual delight. Sky burials feel natural and cyclical, not grim. Every sunrise is a new beauty. Every foal is the future made physical. Every alliance is beauty triumphing over industrial power.

The player's god-eye view through the crystal ball reinforces this: the god watches not to control but to appreciate, to nurture, to witness the herd become something magnificent.

### Hopeful Whimsy

Magic is human technology that has become folklore. A luck necklace pulses faint green. A rusted ring hums ominous chords. Dragons hoard relic artifacts in crude forges built from forgotten factories. The world is strange and beautiful and a little bit dangerous in the way that wilderness is dangerous — not malicious, just indifferent to small creatures.

The exception is Kravathax, who is not indifferent. He is the only thing in the world that actively wants to make it worse.

## Visual & Audio Direction

### World
Vine-draped megastructures. Glowing solar panels half-buried in moss. Crystal rivers running through reclaimed plazas. Bioluminescent flowers on moonlit trails.

### The Herd
Coats shimmering like stained glass. Communal grooming scenes rendered in loving detail. Foals playing in the background of management screens.

### The Threat
Harsh metal clanks against organic hoofbeats. Esroh movement is wrong in a way that's hard to place. Industrial orange glow on the horizon at night. Distant factory roar as a counterpoint to whale-song.

### Victory
Solar flares cleanse factory runoff. Herds gallop through dawn light. The epilogue shows foals in pristine solar fields, the crystal ball glowing warm in the god's unseen hands.
